# Predicting Downstream Nitrate Concentrations in Iowa Waterways

**Research Question:** Given a specified rectangle in the state of Iowa, a value for the average nitrogen surplus in that area, and a date, can we predict the likelihood of observing a nitrate concentration exceeding **10 mg/L** at specific water sites downstream of the specified area within a month of the given date?

---

## Suggested Data Stack

* **Historical Weather Data:** Provided by NOAA / National Weather Service, containing historical precipitation, temperature, and snowpack data. This data helps understand how a nitrogen surplus is transported into the water supply via rainfall. It captures runoff events and the massive flushes of nitrates caused by spring thaws combined with heavy rains. Additionally, soil temperature dictates the microbial activity (nitrification) that converts ammonia into the highly mobile nitrate form.
* **Fertilization Proxies:** Managed via state agricultural departments and local agronomic extensions. Contains county-level fertilizer sales records and general agronomic timelines (e.g., fall anhydrous ammonia applications versus spring pre-plant applications). These proxies track when nitrogen is put back into the soil, taking the place of exact, fine-grained historical fertilization schedules which are difficult to source due to privacy laws. Combined with weather data, this helps estimate when fertilizer was applied and mobilized.
* **Hydrology and Streamflow Data (USGS):** Accessible at `https://www.usgs.gov/tools/water-data-nation-wdfn`, containing continuous historical streamflow (discharge) data for rivers and streams. Predicting concentration requires knowing the volume of water moving through the system, as nitrate concentration is a ratio of mass to volume. This data tracks how a small rain event concentrates nitrates, while a massive flood event dilutes them.
* **Water Quality Sensor Data (IIHR):** Accessible at `https://iwqis.iowawis.org/`, containing real-time and historical continuous time series data for nitrate levels. This is paired with USGS streamflow data to monitor downstream routing and track concentration limits.
* **Subsurface Tile Drainage & Topography (USDA SSURGO):** Accessible at `https://websoilsurvey.nrcs.usda.gov/`, containing soil drainage classifications and permeability metrics. Identifies high-permeability soils equipped with artificial subsurface pipes called "tile drains". This accounts for the massive portion of agricultural runoff that bypasses natural soil filtration and travels directly into ditches and streams beneath the surface.
